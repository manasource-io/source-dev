# Evidence standards

This document defines how evidence quality should influence Manasource resource content.

The purpose is not to eliminate judgment. It is to make judgment more consistent.

## Core principles

- stronger claims require stronger evidence
- direct evidence beats repeated summaries of the same claim
- recency matters, but quality matters more
- mechanistic plausibility is not enough on its own for strong outcome claims
- low-quality or sensational framing should not leak into resource prose

## Source preference hierarchy

Prefer sources in roughly this order when supporting substantive health claims:

1. systematic reviews and meta-analyses
2. major randomized controlled trials
3. strong prospective cohort studies, when trials are impractical or unavailable
4. major guideline or consensus statements from reputable institutions
5. narrative reviews, used carefully
6. high-quality secondary explainers, only when they clearly point back to the primary evidence

Avoid relying on:

- unsourced blog posts
- listicles
- influencer summaries without traceable primary references
- sensational social posts without durable source support

## Credibility tiers

### Strong

Appropriate when evidence is consistent, reasonably direct, and supported by high-quality literature.

Typical examples:

- systematic review or meta-analysis of good studies
- multiple consistent RCTs
- major cohort evidence with strong external consistency

### Moderate

Appropriate when evidence is useful but more limited, narrower, or less consistent.

Typical examples:

- smaller RCT base
- mixed but still meaningful study results
- stronger mechanistic support paired with limited direct outcome evidence

### Emerging

Appropriate when the signal is interesting but still early.

Typical examples:

- small early trials
- preliminary clinical evidence
- promising but immature translational work

### Anecdotal or weak

Appropriate when a claim is mostly based on personal reports, weak summaries, or poor-quality sourcing.

These sources should generally not be used to support substantive resource claims.

## Claim calibration

### Strong evidence can support language like:

- reduces risk
- improves
- increases
- is associated with

### Moderate evidence often calls for language like:

- may improve
- may support
- has shown benefit in some studies

### Emerging evidence should usually use language like:

- early research suggests
- preliminary evidence indicates
- potential benefit remains under investigation

## Mechanistic evidence

Mechanistic explanations are useful for context, but they are not substitutes for outcome evidence.

It is acceptable to describe a plausible mechanism when:

- the mechanism is relevant to the resource
- the distinction between mechanism and real-world outcomes remains clear

Avoid implying that a plausible mechanism proves a clinical effect.

## Secondary sources

Secondary sources can be useful for discovery and orientation, especially when they summarize multiple studies well.

But whenever a resource page is updated, the preferred end state is:

- primary literature or higher-order evidence in `references`
- secondary explainers used sparingly and only when they add editorial value

## Reasons to remove or replace a reference

Replace or remove a reference when it is:

- unrelated to the claim it appears to support
- lower quality than a better available source
- redundant with stronger references already present
- misleadingly framed in the resource text
- no longer available or clearly unstable

## Research leads are not evidence

Posts, threads, and cached lead files are discovery inputs only.
They can point toward evidence, but they are not evidence by themselves.
