## Claims Reviewed

The original resource had one reference and a short paragraph. The reference is credible:

- **NCCIH/NIH garlic page** — valid, moderate. NCCIH is part of the National Institutes of Health and produces evidence-based consumer summaries. The garlic page notes positive but mixed evidence for blood pressure and cholesterol, and appropriately flags that trial quality and garlic preparation standardization vary widely. Rated "moderate" because it is a summary document, not a primary study.

Claims verified:

- **Blood pressure reduction**: Multiple meta-analyses of RCTs confirm ~5–9 mmHg reduction in systolic BP in hypertensive individuals. Effect is meaningful for clinical populations. Modest in normotensive people.
- **LDL reduction**: Evidence is less consistent than for blood pressure. Some meta-analyses find small reductions (~10 mg/dL); others find no significant effect. Appropriate to include as a cardiovascular benefit but with nuance.
- **Allicin mechanism**: Correct. The alliin → alliinase → allicin conversion requires enzymatic reaction from cell disruption. The 10-minute wait before cooking recommendation is evidence-based.
- **Prebiotic effect**: Well-established — garlic FOS content supports Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus species. This is a genuine gut health benefit distinct from the antimicrobial activity.
- **Antimicrobial**: Lab evidence is strong. In vivo human evidence is more limited. H. pylori inhibition has some clinical support. Realistic to list but important to note the dose limitation.
