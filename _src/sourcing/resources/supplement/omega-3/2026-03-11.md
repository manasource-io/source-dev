## Claims Reviewed

The original resource had two references and one sentence. Both references are high-quality:

- **Examine.com fish oil page** — valid, strong. One of the most comprehensive evidence summaries for omega-3, covering mechanisms, dose-response, specific outcomes, and nuance on dietary vs. supplemental sources.
- **Rizos et al. 2012 JAMA (PubMed 23746276)** — valid, strong (with important nuance). This is a well-designed meta-analysis of 20 RCTs (68,680 participants) that found omega-3 supplementation was NOT significantly associated with reduced risk of all-cause mortality, cardiac death, sudden death, MI, or stroke. This is an important check on overclaiming cardiovascular benefits from supplementation.

The original resource's single sentence — "significantly reduce inflammation, support cardiovascular health, and may modestly improve depressive symptoms" — is partially accurate but:

- "significantly reduce inflammation" is well-supported
- "support cardiovascular health" is supported for dietary intake and triglyceride reduction specifically, but oversimplified given the Rizos meta-analysis
- "modestly improve depressive symptoms" is accurate

Claims verified:

- **Triglyceride reduction**: FDA-recognized, very well-established. 15–30% reduction at 2–4g/day in elevated-triglyceride populations. One of the most reliable effects of omega-3 supplementation.
- **Anti-inflammatory**: EPA and DHA's conversion to resolvins and protectins is mechanistically well-characterized. CRP/IL-6 reductions in RCTs are consistent.
- **Brain health**: DHA's role as a structural component of neuronal membranes is established biochemistry. Clinical evidence for supplementation improving cognitive performance is positive but effect sizes are modest.
- **Depression**: EPA-rich formulations show consistent benefit in meta-analyses of clinical depression populations. Not effective for non-clinical populations. Evidence supports adjunct role.
- **Cardiovascular nuance**: Correctly represented — dietary fish strongly associated with CV benefit; supplemental omega-3 evidence is less clear. REDUCE-IT findings for high-dose EPA are noted but debated due to mineral oil placebo concern.
