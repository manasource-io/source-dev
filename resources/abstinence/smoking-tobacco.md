Tobacco smoking is the leading preventable cause of death worldwide, responsible for approximately 6–8 million premature deaths per year globally, and contributing to over 175 million deaths and 4.3 billion years of life lost between 1990 and 2021. It causes harm across nearly every organ system , not just through the well-known risks of lung cancer and emphysema, but through cardiovascular disease, stroke, at least 14 cancers, type 2 diabetes, osteoporosis, reproductive harm, immune suppression, and accelerated biological aging. Nicotine's addictive properties are comparable to those of heroin and cocaine, making cessation genuinely difficult , but the health benefits of stopping are among the most dramatic and well-quantified in all of medicine.

## Life Expectancy {#life-expectancy}

The life expectancy impact of lifelong smoking is stark: smokers lose an average of **10–11 years of life** compared to never-smokers. This is not a marginal statistical finding but a consistent pattern replicated across dozens of large cohort studies in multiple countries. The good news is equally clear: cessation reverses much of this loss. People who quit smoking at ages **25–34 recover approximately 10 years of life**; those who quit at 35–44 recover about 9 years; those who quit at 45–54 recover about 6 years. Even quitting at age **65 yields 1.4–3.7 additional years** depending on sex. A 2024 GBD systematic analysis (Lancet Public Health) modeled that a global tobacco elimination scenario would prevent 2.04 billion cumulative years of life lost by 2050 , among the largest potential public health gains available.

## All-Cause Mortality {#all-cause-mortality}

Current smokers have approximately **twice the all-cause mortality rate** of never-smokers, translating to a mortality rate advancement of roughly 6 years. Former smokers have about 1.3× the mortality of never-smokers , meaning cessation eliminates a large portion of the excess risk, though not all. A 2022 study (JAMA Network Open) following 551,388 US adults found that greater all-cause mortality in smokers versus never-smokers was consistent across all racial, ethnic, and sex groups , smoking is not a risk factor that spares any demographic. The excess mortality in former smokers decreases with time since cessation: those who quit fewer than 10 years ago had a mortality advance of 3.9 years; those who quit 20+ years ago had an advance of just 0.7 years, closely approaching the never-smoker baseline.

## Cardiovascular Health {#cardiovascular-health}

Cardiovascular disease is the largest single category of smoking-attributable death. Smoking damages the endothelium, promotes atherosclerosis, increases blood clotting tendency, raises blood pressure and heart rate, and reduces oxygen-carrying capacity of blood through carbon monoxide. The cardiovascular recovery timeline after cessation is more rapid than most people expect: within **20 minutes** of the last cigarette, heart rate and blood pressure begin to fall. Within **1 year**, the excess risk of coronary heart disease is cut in half. Within **10–15 years**, CVD risk approaches that of a never-smoker. This rapid cardiovascular recovery is one of the most compelling arguments for quitting at any age.

## Cancer Risk {#cancer-risk}

Smoking causes at least 14 cancers, with lung cancer being the most strongly linked , accounting for approximately 85% of all lung cancer cases. The lung cancer risk reduction after cessation is substantial but slower than cardiovascular recovery: after **10 years of abstinence**, lung cancer risk falls to approximately 50% of that of continuing smokers. Bladder, kidney, esophageal, head and neck, cervical, and pancreatic cancer risks also decline progressively with years of cessation. Colorectal and gastric cancer risks follow similar trajectories. The biological basis for these reductions is well-understood: removal of carcinogenic smoke exposure allows DNA repair mechanisms to correct accumulated damage, reduces chronic inflammation, and allows normal cellular turnover to gradually replace smoke-damaged cells with healthy ones.

**On cessation:** Unaided quit attempts succeed long-term only about 5% of the time , not a failure of willpower, but a consequence of nicotine's powerful effects on dopaminergic reward circuits. Effective cessation support dramatically improves odds: **varenicline (Champix/Chantix)** combined with behavioral support achieves abstinence rates exceeding 40% at 52 weeks. Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT , patches, gum, lozenges) roughly doubles unaided quit success. Brief physician advice alone increases cessation rates by 1–3 percentage points , enough to have meaningful population-level impact. Combination NRT (a long-acting patch plus a short-acting form for cravings) outperforms single NRT. The evidence is clear: treating nicotine addiction as a medical condition and seeking evidence-based cessation support is the most reliable path to quitting.
