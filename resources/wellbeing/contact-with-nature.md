Contact with nature encompasses a broad range of exposures: time spent in forests, parks, or countryside; views of trees or water from windows; gardening; coastal environments; and urban green space. It has been studied through multiple theoretical lenses , Attention Restoration Theory (natural environments restore directed attention capacity depleted by demanding tasks), Stress Recovery Theory (natural environments trigger faster physiological recovery from acute stress), and biophilia (an evolved human affinity for living systems that supports wellbeing). The effects are measured physiologically (cortisol, heart rate, blood pressure, NK cells) and psychologically (mood, anxiety, depression scales), providing converging evidence across both domains.

## Stress Reduction {#stress-reduction}

The most detailed physiological evidence comes from Japanese research on Shinrin-Yoku (forest bathing). A 2017 systematic review covering 64 studies conducted in Japan and China found consistent evidence that forest immersion versus urban environments increased parasympathetic nervous system activity (including an 80% increase in parasympathetic HRV indicators in one large study), reduced salivary cortisol, lowered heart rate, and decreased blood pressure , particularly in hypertensive individuals. These effects were observed during sessions as short as 15–20 minutes and persisted for hours after leaving the forest environment. The physiological mechanism involves reduced activation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and the sympathetic "fight-or-flight" response, with natural environments appearing to engage involuntary attention rather than the effortful directed attention that drives stress arousal.

## Mental Health {#mental-health}

A 2024 meta-analysis (59 studies) specifically examining green space exposure and psychiatric disorders found that greater access to and use of green spaces was associated with an overall **9% reduction in psychiatric disorder odds** (OR = 0.91, 95% CI 0.89–0.92) across depression, anxiety, dementia, schizophrenia, and ADHD. The effects were most consistent for depression and anxiety. A 2024 Navigation Guide meta-analysis found that access to gardens was associated with 29% lower odds of depression (OR = 0.71) and 27% lower odds of anxiety (OR = 0.73) during the COVID-19 pandemic. Research on dose-response suggests that **at least 30 minutes per week** of nature contact may yield the clearest mental health benefits, with one estimate projecting 7% fewer cases of depression and 9% fewer cases of hypertension if minimum exposure thresholds were met population-wide.

## Cardiovascular Health {#cardiovascular-health}

A landmark 2019 systematic review and meta-analysis (Twohig-Bennett & Jones, *Environmental Research*) synthesizing 103 observational studies and 40 interventional studies examining approximately 100 health outcomes found that greenspace exposure was associated with significantly **lower diastolic blood pressure** (-1.97 mmHg), reduced heart rate, lower salivary cortisol, and reduced risk of type 2 diabetes, all-cause mortality, and cardiovascular mortality. The blood pressure reduction, while modest in absolute terms, is clinically meaningful at a population level , comparable to the effect of a low-sodium diet or modest aerobic exercise program. The cardiovascular benefits appear to operate partly through the stress pathway (lower cortisol → lower sympathetic tone → lower blood pressure) and partly through increased physical activity associated with nature use.

## Immune Function {#immune-function}

Trees and other plants emit volatile organic compounds called phytoncides (primarily alpha-pinene and beta-pinene), which have been shown in controlled Japanese studies to increase natural killer (NK) cell count and activity in humans following forest exposure. NK cells are a key component of innate immune surveillance , they identify and kill virus-infected cells and cancer cells. A series of studies by Qing Li found that overnight forest trips increased NK cell activity by 50% on the day after the trip, and that this elevation persisted for 7 days in female nurses. Isolated phytoncide inhalation in hotel rooms produced similar (if smaller) effects, suggesting the compound itself , not just exercise or relaxation , contributes to the immune response. The practical significance for cancer surveillance or infection resistance is not yet quantified in large trials.

**Practical guidance:** The evidence supports meaningful benefit from relatively modest nature exposure. A minimum of **20–30 minutes in a natural environment 2–3 times per week** is supported by the research for stress and mental health benefits. "Nature" does not require wilderness: urban parks, tree-lined streets, riverside paths, and even views of trees or water from windows produce measurable effects. Gardening provides a particularly rich form of nature contact combining exposure to soil microbes (which may have independent mood-modulating effects via the vagus nerve), physical activity, and visual engagement with living systems. The dose-response continues to improve beyond minimums, but even small increases in green space access and use produce measurable population-level effects on stress and mood.
