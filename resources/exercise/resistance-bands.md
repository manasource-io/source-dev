Resistance band training uses elastic bands to provide variable, progressive resistance through a full range of motion. A systematic review and meta-analysis confirmed that elastic resistance training produces strength gains comparable to conventional resistance training (free weights, machines) across both upper and lower body exercises , a finding that validates bands as a legitimate primary resistance training tool rather than merely a supplement to gym work.

The key mechanical difference from free weights is the resistance profile: bands provide minimal resistance at the start of a movement (when muscles are longest and weakest) and maximal resistance at peak contraction (when muscles are shortest and strongest). This accommodating resistance pattern matches the natural strength curve of many exercises , creating a different but effective training stimulus. The continuous tension throughout the movement also prevents the "momentum" that can reduce free weight effectiveness at the top of lifts.

For rehabilitation and older adult use, resistance bands offer particular advantages: the lower joint compressive forces compared to heavy free weights reduce pain and injury risk in arthritic or recovering joints, while still providing sufficient mechanical load to stimulate muscle growth and strength adaptation. Bands can be anchored to doors, poles, or fixed objects, enabling exercises for every major muscle group without specialized equipment , making them one of the most genuinely accessible forms of resistance training.

Practical implementation requires a set of bands at varying resistance levels (light, medium, heavy), typically color-coded by manufacturer. A complete full-body resistance training program can be performed in 30 minutes with four to six band exercises. As strength increases, progression is achieved by using thicker bands, shortening the band length, slowing the eccentric phase, or adding reps before moving to a higher resistance.
