Lemon juice offers a targeted set of evidence-supported benefits centered on its vitamin C content and citric acid profile. A fresh lemon provides approximately 30–50mg of vitamin C , a meaningful share of the 65–90mg daily recommended intake. Vitamin C is an essential antioxidant and cofactor for collagen synthesis, immune function, and the conversion of non-heme (plant-based) iron into a bioavailable form. Adding lemon juice to iron-rich plant foods , spinach, lentils, fortified cereals , meaningfully increases iron absorption, which is particularly important for people following plant-based diets where non-heme iron is the primary dietary source.

Citric acid in lemon juice provides a clinically recognized benefit for kidney stone prevention. Citrate , the form citric acid takes once metabolized , inhibits calcium oxalate crystal formation by binding urinary calcium and preventing crystal growth. A daily intake of approximately 4 oz (125ml) of pure lemon juice has been shown to significantly raise urinary citrate levels, producing effects comparable to pharmacological potassium citrate therapy for hypocitraturic calcium stone formers. This is specific and well-established enough that urologists refer to it as "lemonade therapy."

One widely repeated claim , that lemon juice has a general "alkalizing effect" on the body , should be understood narrowly. The body tightly regulates blood pH within a range of 7.35–7.45 regardless of diet; dietary choices do not meaningfully shift systemic pH. What is accurate is that citrate metabolism raises urinary pH and citrate excretion, which is specifically protective against kidney stone formation , a real and targeted effect, not a systemic alkalizing of body chemistry.

Vitamin C is heat- and oxygen-sensitive, so bottled and pasteurized lemon juice contains significantly less than fresh-squeezed. For vitamin C and kidney stone prevention purposes, fresh juice is preferred. At high intake levels, citric acid may aggravate acid reflux in susceptible individuals. Some medications , blood pressure drugs, diabetes medications, and certain statins , may interact with citrus components at high doses.
