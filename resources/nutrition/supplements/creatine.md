Creatine is one of the best-studied supplements in sports nutrition, with decades of research supporting its role in improving physical performance and emerging evidence for cognitive benefits.

## Mechanism

Creatine is stored in muscle tissue as phosphocreatine, which replenishes ATP (the cell's primary energy currency) during high-intensity exercise. By increasing phosphocreatine availability, supplemental creatine enables muscles to sustain peak power output slightly longer before fatigue sets in. This effect is most pronounced in short-burst, anaerobic activities , sprinting, weightlifting, and explosive sports.

## Strength and Power {#strength-and-power}

Multiple randomized controlled trials and meta-analyses confirm that creatine monohydrate increases maximal strength and peak power output across resistance-training populations. Gains of 5–15% in strength measures compared to placebo are consistently reported. Effects accumulate over weeks of supplementation combined with consistent training, and are dose-responsive within typical supplementation ranges.

## Muscle Recovery {#muscle-recovery}

By accelerating ATP regeneration, creatine reduces the decline in muscle function between sets and between training sessions, enabling greater total training volume. Creatine also attenuates some markers of exercise-induced muscle damage in certain trials, though results across studies are mixed.

## Cognitive Support {#cognitive-support}

The brain relies heavily on ATP for neurotransmission and cellular maintenance. A systematic review of six RCTs found evidence that creatine supplementation improves short-term memory and intelligence/reasoning in healthy adults, with the greatest effects seen under metabolic stress , sleep deprivation, aging, and low dietary creatine intake (e.g., vegetarian diets). Effects on other cognitive domains (attention, processing speed) remain less consistently demonstrated.

## Aging and Longevity Applications {#aging}

A growing body of 2024–2025 research positions creatine as a meaningful tool for healthy aging beyond athletic populations. A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis (20 studies, 1,093 older adults) found that creatine plus exercise training significantly improved one-rep max strength and reduced body fat percentage compared to exercise alone. A 2025 narrative review in the Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition concluded that creatine monohydrate is safe for older adults and clinical populations, with well-documented benefits for lean body mass, muscle strength, functional ability, glucose kinetics, and memory , with potential application in age-related sarcopenia, osteoporosis, frailty, and metabolic disorders.

Cognition-specific evidence in older adults is emerging. A 2025 systematic review (6 studies, 1,542 participants) found that 83% of studies reported a positive relationship between creatine supplementation and cognition in older adults, particularly for memory and attention. Evidence quality was mixed, and the authors called for more rigorous clinical trials. A 2025 Alzheimer's pilot trial (CABA trial, 19 participants, 8 weeks at 20 g/day) found an 11% increase in brain total creatine and significant improvements on global and fluid cognitive composites , feasibility results that warrant a full-scale RCT.

A separate 2025 finding noted that creatine synthesis and dietary intake decline with age and may interfere with DNA methylation patterns associated with biological aging and mortality risk, suggesting a potential longevity mechanism beyond muscle and brain energy metabolism.

## Dosing and Safety

A standard dose is 3–5 g/day of creatine monohydrate taken daily. A loading phase (20 g/day split across 4 doses for 5–7 days) saturates muscle stores faster, but the same endpoint is reached with gradual supplementation. Monohydrate is the most researched form; alternative forms (HCl, ethyl ester) offer no documented advantage at equivalent doses.

Creatine monohydrate is widely considered safe for healthy adults. Common concerns about kidney damage or dehydration have not been substantiated in controlled research. Mild water retention in muscle tissue is expected and reflects proper uptake rather than a side effect.
